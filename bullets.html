signs of a healthy architecture

  • but how do we know that? - how can we tell if an application has a strong foundation?
  • often times just look at characteristics of the application
  • good architecture will produce applications that are:
    • reliable - they behave as expected
      • reliable applications have no single point of failure
      • so for example the body can survive without its appendix or with a single kidney - it can function without all 4 limbs & even survive brain damage
      • the body can undergo an exceptional amount of stress without the entire system shutting down
      • reliable applications are also autonomic - know how to handle and recover from errors - similar to how bodies can heal themselves after injuries
      • think of all cuts/scrapes/bruises from playing as a kid - imagine if our body didn't know how to recover from those - we'd all look pretty scary
    • flexible - another quality of these applications is that they can easily adapt to changing circumstances
      • typical web app might have to adjust behavior for different environments to work as expected
      • its responding to external conditions - an external condition our bodies adapt to might be the weather
      • cold might get goosebumps on our skin - but as soon as we go back inside where its warm, these are no longer needed and they go away
      • flexible apps can also gracefully handle internal changes - they are easy to iterate on and malleable enough to suit extensive changes in logic
      • lots of examples of internal flexibility in bodies - interesting one is the brains ability to rewire itself after damage
      • the flexibility of the brain is referred to as neuroplasticity. theres a rare but effective surgery that can be done on children to relieve seizure disorders by removing half of the brain. its called a hemispherectomy and the problematic half of the brain is cut out and you live your life with half a brain.
    • testable - each part of the application can be tested as an independent unit
      • this helps prevent us from breaking functionality
      • also makes easier to track down and troubleshoot errors when they do occur
      • when you are sick or have pain somewhere in your body - go to the doctor - the reason they can diagnose the problem is bc bodies are testable
      • we can run blood tests and take urine samples & get imaging of particular body parts to narrow down and isolate the source of the problem

architectural affairs

  • have all these great qualities evident in our bodies - but in order to foster theme, arch must follow some key principles to solve right problems
  • arch concerns can vary app to app but there are a couple of really definitive ones that stand out most
  • SOC - app responsibilities are organized into separate layers that overlap as little as possible
    • reduces dependencies between them
    • can see this in practice by looking at diff systems of our bodies and how our bodies responsibilities are distributed across them
    • we have skeletal system, muscular system, cardiovascular system and a bunch of others.
    • all have in common each take on very specialized role in our bodies operations
    • if we deconstruct these systems further, we'll reveal some additional principles behind app arch
  • SR/LK - lets look at the cardiovascular system - the main organ here is the heart.
    • its an important one. if you were going to say the body had a single source of failure, the heart would be it.
    • luckily doctors have found a way to perform heart transplants. last year in the US alone there were more than ___ of these procedures performed.
    • so medical field making many advancements in this area - i have a short clip here for us to watch about a 61-year-old patient, Rob Evans
    • who suffered from cardiomyopathy, his heart was failing and he was admitted to UCLA med center for a transplant
    • WATCH CLIP
    • so besides the very talented doctors and technology, what is it about the actual organ that makes this possible?
    • we literally just saw a heart beating inside of a box without a care in the world like it was totally normal
    • two principles are at play here: single responsibility and least knowledge principles
    • the heart has one very specific function - to pump blood. to expand and contract. thats it. single responsibility.
    • the heart also requires very little information about its environment or surrounding context. clearly it had no idea that it was in a box instead of a body, and pumping blood into plastic tubes instead of veins. it doesnt care.
    • these two principles aid in reusability within our applications.
    • the less work a module does, and less info it needs to know, easier it is to drop it into another page or another application and have it work
    • this heart is being reused.
  • Communication Interface - so assuming our other organs are equally as modular and specific, we have very loose coupling throughout our bodies
    • which is why another goal of app architecture is to provide a standard interface for communication
    • need a way for these highly-specialized pieces of our applications to interact with one another
    • in humans, all of the communication between different systems and organs is done through neurotransmissions, which involves sending electrical impulses and chemical transmissions between neurons in the nervous system
    • we'll look into this more in a little bit

mediator

  • pub/sub pattern that sends/receives messages w/ a common data model
  • encapsulates how diff parts of an app interact
  • can be thought of as a central point of control
  • ever built a backbone app you may have had to write some sort of event dispatcher to send/receive messages that affect multiple views - similar concept
  • in context of bodies, CNS acts as meiator for all different systems in our body to communicate with each other
  • made up of brain and spinal cord - is constantly processing information from various parts of our body to excite or inhibit behavior
  • information isn't sent directly to the CNS however, instead, it first passes through a facade

facade

  • we want an abstracted layer of application to sit between our mediator and our modules
  • so that we can provide a consistent & predictable interface for modules to interact with
  • assuming our application has succeeded in separating its concerns and avoiding tight coupling, the facade is the only part of the application that modules must be aware of
  • in our bodies, this role is played by our PNS
  • PNS sends and receives data from various systems in our body to our CNS and vice versa
  • as i mentioned earlier, communication is achieved through chemical transmissions and electric impulses
  • the CNS is familiar with this type of message and has the ability to process it and react to it if necessary

pros & cons

  • advantage - allows modules to broadcast/listen for messages w/o concern for rest of the system - single connection to mediator rather than multiple to eachother - is one system is damaged, it doesn't bring down all of them
  • disadvantage - difficult to gracefully recover to damage to mediator
  • spinal cord injuries can be so detrimental and life changing - even though all other systems work, if messaging is damaged, theyll never be put to use
  • some of the cells in CNS are so specialized they cant heal themselves like our other organs and tissues might - makes it difficult for doctors to recreate the CNS as it was before an injury
